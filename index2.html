The Lost Islands
Dig up the 1848 Maryland land survey, and you’ll find Sharps Island.

We’re losing land in the Chesapeake Bay.

Sea levels in the Chesapeake have risen a foot in the last century, more than twice the global average. In Maryland and Virginia, that water isn’t just encroaching on coastline; it’s swallowing islands whole.

And with climate change causing sea levels to rise faster than ever, more islands are in danger of vanishing.

What We've Lost

Experts estimate hundreds of islands like Sharps have eroded away since the English explored the bay region in the 1600s.

The natural erosion that the area experiences began accelerating in the 1850s — the same time the Industrial Revolution massively increased our output of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases, said Kristin Reilly, communications director for the National Wildlife Federation.

Since then, upward of 500 islands have sunk beneath the waters of the bay. Some are only specks on old maps or lines in early documents dating to the English settlers, and with holes in the historical record, it’s hard to know just how many we’ve lost, Reilly said. Most islands have never housed people, but they have served an essential role in the bay’s ecosystem, providing habitats for waterfowl and birds.

Using historical shoreline surveys collected by the Maryland Geological Survey, we can track the disappearance of some of those islands.

What's Left to Lose

Those islands that have survived have shrunk drastically. The Tangier Islands off the Virginia coast, still home to more than 700 people as of 2013, has lost two-thirds of their land mass since 1850. A study in Nature estimates the islands will lose their remaining acreage in the next century, and the population will have to evacuate within 50 years.

The Chesapeake Bay is particularly vulnerable to rising sea levels. The land beneath the bay is gradually sinking, a long-term effect of the crater that helped form the bay. The sea is rising faster in the Chesapeake than any area on North America’s Atlantic coast. Experts estimate water in the bay could rise another three to four feet by 2100.
